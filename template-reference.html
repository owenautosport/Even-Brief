Wall Street steadies on rotation out of tech; oil and gold fall, Micron jumps after the close

Verified— US, German and Japanese closing figures are the 24 June close, corroborated across two or more outlets; the FTSE 100's small move and some European single-stock figures vary by source and are labelled

U.S. equities closed mixed on Wednesday 24 June as investors rotated out of technology and AI-linked names into consumer and healthcare sectors. The S&P 500 eased 0.10% to 7,358.22 and the Nasdaq Composite slipped 0.43% to about 25,476.60, while the Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 0.35% to 51,848.90 and the Russell 2000 added about 0.37% (all 24 June close).

The week's memory-chip-led decline steadied rather than deepening. After the close, Micron Technology rose about 15% in after-hours trading after reporting fiscal third-quarter revenue of roughly $41.5 billion and adjusted earnings of $25.11 a share, both above analyst estimates, and guiding the current quarter to about $50 billion. The results eased some concern about the pace of AI-infrastructure spending.

In Asia, Japan's Nikkei 225 closed down 0.88% at 69,174.97, while the Shanghai Composite ended little changed, up about 0.11% near 4,111 (24 June). In Europe, Germany's DAX closed down about 0.98% at 24,893.58 and the pan-regional Stoxx 600 finished modestly lower. London's FTSE 100 was little changed, closing around 10,429, with sources placing it between a small gain and a 0.09% fall; the property developer Segro rose sharply after a takeover approach.

Commodities fell as Middle East tensions eased. Brent crude dropped about 3.5% to around $75.5 a barrel and WTI fell roughly 3–4% to near $71, around their lowest in months, after Washington's 60-day arrangement on Iranian oil and as Strait of Hormuz traffic recovered. Spot gold fell about 3% to around $3,970–4,000 an ounce as the dollar index reached a 13-month high.

The macro backdrop remains a Fed leaning toward higher rates. At its 16–17 June meeting the FOMC held its benchmark rate at 3.5%–3.75% unanimously, dropped language biased toward cuts, and lifted its end-2026 median projection to about 3.8% from 3.4%, citing energy-driven inflation. On 24 June, Germany's Ifo business-climate index, U.S. new-home-sales data and weekly EIA crude inventories were among the releases.

What's next: after Micron's results, attention turns to the Federal Reserve's bank stress-test results on 25 June and the U.S. core PCE price index — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — on 26 June. Investors said they would watch whether the rotation out of chips persists and how memory pricing and high-bandwidth-memory demand develop into 2027. Micron's after-hours move had not been confirmed by a regular-session close at the time of writing.

Climate Health

UK provisionally records its hottest June day as red heat warnings hit western Europe

Developing— the Met Office record and the red warnings are confirmed by the Met Office, UKHSA and multiple outlets; the headline temperature is provisional and individual station figures are subject to verification

The United Kingdom provisionally recorded its hottest June day on Wednesday, 24 June 2026, the Met Office said, as a Red Extreme Heat Warning covered parts of southern England and red alerts were issued in several other western European countries. The agency said the heat would peak on Thursday, 25 June, with the red warning remaining in force into Thursday evening.

The Met Office said a provisional high of about 36°C was reached at Wisley in Surrey and at Gosport in Hampshire. The previous June record had been passed earlier in the day, first at Charlwood in Surrey and then at Wiggonholt in West Sussex, the agency said. The figures are provisional and subject to quality control before they are confirmed.

A Red Extreme Heat Warning — the Met Office's highest heat warning level — was in force for parts of central, southern and eastern England, including Surrey, from 09:00 on 24 June to 21:00 on 25 June. The Met Office said it was only the second red extreme-heat warning it had issued, and that temperatures could reach 37–40°C in places on Thursday.

The UK Health Security Agency issued a Red heat-health alert for South-East England and several other regions, in force from 01:00 on 24 June to 23:00 on 25 June. The agency said the heat was likely to affect people of all ages and to increase pressure on health and social-care services. UKHSA has issued only one previous red heat-health alert, in July 2022.

Forecasters attributed the heat to a high-pressure system drawing hot air northward from continental Europe and north Africa, combined with clear skies and high June sun. Overnight temperatures in parts of the South East were forecast to stay above 20°C, conditions the Met Office described as "tropical nights."

Elsewhere in Europe, France's national weather service, Météo-France, placed a large share of the country under its highest heat alert during the week, and a temperature of 44.3°C was reported at Pissos, in the Landes, on 23 June. Italy issued red heat alerts for a number of cities, including Rome and Milan, and authorities across the region warned of risks to health and to outdoor workers.

Why it matters: the Met Office and UKHSA said extreme heat raises the risk of illness and death, particularly among older people and those with existing health conditions, and can disrupt transport, energy and water services. Scientists have said that heatwaves are becoming more frequent and intense as the global climate warms; attribution of any single event requires separate study.

What comes next: the Met Office said the heat was expected to peak on Thursday before breaking down from the west later in the week, with thundery showers possible as cooler air moves in. Confirmed record figures and any health impacts were expected to be assessed in the days afterwards.

US and Iran agree Hormuz safe-passage line; IMO to evacuate 11,000 seafarers

Verified— the de-confliction cell, the Hormuz line and the IMO evacuation are each reported by two or more independent outlets plus the IMO statement; some operational details remain thin

Follow-up to earlier coverage

The United States and Iran agreed to establish a communication line intended to ensure the safe passage of commercial vessels through the Strait of Hormuz and a "de-confliction cell" covering Lebanon, mediators Qatar and Pakistan said following talks in Switzerland. The arrangements were set out in a joint statement issued by the two mediating states after the negotiating round near Lake Lucerne on 21 June 2026.

According to the mediators, the Hormuz channel is intended "to avoid incidents and miscommunication with the aim of safe passage for commercial vessels," operating within a 60-day window outlined in a US–Iran road map toward a final agreement. The same statement described a de-confliction cell involving the United States, Iran and Lebanon, facilitated by Qatar and Pakistan, to support "the termination of military operations in Lebanon."

Iran's foreign minister, Abbas Araghchi, called the Lebanon de-confliction cell the "first real test" of the process that emerged from the Switzerland talks. US Vice-President JD Vance said the discussions had set a "good foundation," telling reporters on 22 June 2026: "The final deal is the house. We set the foundation. We haven't built the house, but we've laid a successful foundation."

Separately, the International Maritime Organization announced on 23 June 2026 a plan to evacuate about 11,000 seafarers who remain stranded in the region. The UN agency said the operation would be carried out with Iran, Oman, other coastal states, the United States and the maritime industry.

Trump cancels housing-bill signing, conditioning it on an elections bill

Verified— the cancelled signing and Trump's statement are confirmed by CBS, CNN, NPR and HousingWire; the underlying vote counts and provisions are corroborated across NPR, NBC, CNBC and TIME

President Donald Trump on 24 June 2026 cancelled a planned ceremony to sign the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act, saying he would not sign the bipartisan housing bill until Congress passed a separate elections measure. The announcement came less than two hours before a scheduled news conference and signing, according to CBS News and CNN.

In a post on social media, Trump said: "Today's Housing News Conference and Signing is hereby cancelled until such time as we pass the desperately needed SAVE AMERICA ACT, which I consider to be a National Emergency." The SAVE America Act is a Republican-backed elections bill that would impose new requirements on voter registration and ballot casting. Trump later declined to say whether he would veto the housing bill, according to CNN.

The U.S. House of Representatives had passed the 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act by a vote of 358-32, sending the bill to the president. All 32 votes against were cast by Republicans, while Democrats present voted in favour, according to NPR and NBC News.

The Senate cleared the same measure by 85-5. The five senators who voted against were all Republicans: Ron Johnson of Wisconsin, Mike Lee of Utah, Rand Paul of Kentucky, Rick Scott of Florida and Tommy Tuberville of Alabama, according to TIME. Among the 85 votes in favour were 41 Republicans and 44 Democrats.

"ROAD" stands for "Renewing Opportunity in the American Dream." The legislation was developed by Senate Banking Committee chairman Tim Scott (R-S.C.) and ranking member Elizabeth Warren (D-Mass.), with House Financial Services Committee chairman French Hill (R-Ark.) and ranking member Maxine Waters (D-Calif.).

The bill includes more than 45 provisions. A section titled "Homes Are For People, Not Corporations" restricts purchases of single-family homes by large institutional investors that directly or indirectly own at least 350 single-family homes, with exemptions for investors building or buying new homes for the rental market, according to the Bipartisan Policy Center.

Other provisions include a $200 million annual competitive grant program for local governments and tribes, an FHA pilot for mortgages under $100,000, an expansion of the Rental Assistance Demonstration program cap by 100,000 units, and streamlined federal environmental reviews for housing. NBC News reported the package gives local governments incentives to revise zoning rules to allow more homebuilding.

Study identifies why H5N1 infects dairy cows' udders rather than their lungs

Verified— the journal, lead author and receptor mechanism are reported consistently by the University of Pittsburgh release, EurekAlert, ScienceDaily and Phys.org

Researchers at the University of Pittsburgh School of Public Health have reported a mechanistic explanation for why H5N1 avian influenza infects dairy cows' udders rather than their lungs. The study was published in the journal Science Advances on 19 June 2026.

When H5N1 began infecting U.S. dairy cattle in 2024, the disease looked different from the respiratory illness the virus causes in other mammals: it produced severe infection in the cows' udders and largely spared the lungs, which made it difficult for veterinarians to recognise at first.

Using binding experiments, staining methods and high-resolution imaging, the team found that not all of the cows' sialic acid receptors behaved the same way. Only a subtype known as N-linked sialic acid receptors could bind H5N1. Those receptors were nearly absent in the cows' airway tissue but widespread in udder tissue.

The lead author, Suresh Kuchipudi, chair of infectious diseases and microbiology at the school, said the finding helps explain the unusual pathology. "When the real culprit turned out to be bird flu, everyone in the field was caught completely by surprise," he said.

Researchers searching the interstellar object 3I/ATLAS for radio signals that could indicate extraterrestrial technology have reported a null result, finding no transmissions beyond known sources of human-made interference. The object remains, by scientific consensus, a natural comet.

The principal search was carried out with the Allen Telescope Array, a 42-dish radio interferometer at the Hat Creek Radio Observatory in northern California, operated by the SETI Institute. The team observed 3I/ATLAS for a total of 7.25 hours across five sessions in July 2025, shortly after the object's discovery, scanning frequencies from 1 to 9 gigahertz for narrowband signals of the kind a transmitter might produce.

The search initially flagged nearly 74 million narrowband "hits." After filtering for radio-frequency interference and applying localisation checks, about 211 candidates remained for visual inspection. None were judged worthy of follow-up, and all were consistent with human-generated interference. The study, led by Sofia Z. Sheikh of the SETI Institute, was published in The Astronomical Journal in June 2026.

The null result allowed the team to place upper limits on any radio transmitter operating on or near the object, ruling out narrowband transmitters with an effective radiated power of roughly 10 to 110 watts — comparable to common household electronics.

How this briefing works

Every edition is written like a newswire — flat, sourced and impartial — then layered with tools that show you how solid each story is and how differently it's being reported. Here's what each feature means.

Getting around
☰

The menu

Open the menu (top-left) to jump between sections. Top Stories expands to every story in the edition; Markets expands to the markets pages; Display settings sit at the bottom. The bar at the top always shows which section you're reading.

★

Top Headlines

The landing page: a lead story plus a clickable card for every other story. Tap any card to open the full article.

🌧

Weather & radar

The masthead weather panel has today's hourly temperature chart joined onto it. The full Weather page (menu or top-bar tab) adds today's temperature & precipitation charts, a tap-through 7-day forecast, any Met Office warnings, and a large animated rain radar (the radar needs an internet connection).

🔎

Search & archive

Browse and search every story across editions by keyword, or filter by topic. Today's stories open in full; earlier ones open as a summary card. The archive grows with each new edition.

How we show reliability
◉

Confidence meter

At the top of each story, three pips and a label rate how settled the facts are.

Verified— core facts agree across multiple independent sources
Developing— fast-moving or partly single-sourced
Disputed— sources materially conflict on key facts
✅

Fact-check tags

Individual claims are marked ✅ Confirmed (corroborated across independent sources) or 🔍 Unverified (single-origin or still moving). The sidebar's Fact-Check box summarises them per story.

⚠️

Logic flags

Where numbers, timelines or claims don't add up, a numbered Logic Flags box explains the discrepancy, names the conflicting sources, and says what would resolve it. When everything checks out, it says so too.

⚖️

Common Ground vs Contested

For contested stories, a box separates the facts all sides agree on from the points still in dispute.

Seeing the spin
📰

Media Framing & bias bar

On politically contentious stories, a section mid-article shows how the coverage breaks down. The bar is sized to the share of sources on each side — left, centre, right — and each outlet is mapped to its published lean (e.g. from AllSides). It then summarises, even-handedly, how each side frames the same events. Splits are approximate.
