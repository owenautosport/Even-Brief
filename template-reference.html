Twin earthquakes near Caracas kill at least 164, Venezuela's acting president says

Developing— core facts (magnitudes, sequence, location, US and Chinese responses) are multi-sourced, but the casualty tally is rising and single-sourced to the government

Two earthquakes struck northern Venezuela on the evening of Wednesday 24 June 2026, killing at least 164 people and injuring 971, acting President Delcy Rodríguez said early Thursday. The figures were reported by CNN, Al Jazeera, NPR and NBC News and remained the official government count as of 25 June.

The United States Geological Survey (USGS) recorded a magnitude 7.2 foreshock followed 39 seconds later by a magnitude 7.5 mainshock. The USGS placed the larger event near Yumare in Yaracuy state, roughly 100 miles (about 160 km) west of Caracas, at shallow depth; an initial reading of M7.1 was revised to M7.2 and the second event put at M7.5.

Rodríguez said her office received reports of building collapses across Caracas and several states, including Carabobo, Aragua, Miranda, Trujillo and La Guaira. She described the coastal state of La Guaira, north of the capital, as a "disaster zone", and said its reported casualties were not yet included in the national count.

Infrastructure was disrupted across the affected region. Reports cited by Al Jazeera said Simón Bolívar International Airport, which serves Caracas, was damaged and flights were cancelled; the Caracas Metro and rail services were suspended; and power and telecommunications failed in parts of the country. Rodríguez said school classes were suspended nationwide.

China, a major buyer of Venezuelan oil, said it would assist; Rodríguez said offers of aid had also come from Brazil, El Salvador, Ecuador, Panama, France, Switzerland and Caribbean nations.

The USGS PAGER system issued a red alert for both events, its highest level, indicating that high casualties and extensive damage are probable. Its modelling estimated a 39% probability of 1,000–10,000 deaths and a 37% probability of 10,000–100,000 deaths, and projected economic losses most likely between $10 billion and $100 billion. The 164 confirmed deaths reflect only counts relayed by authorities so far.

Why it matters: The M7.5 mainshock is, according to USGS data cited by multiple outlets, the strongest earthquake to strike Venezuela since a magnitude 7.6–7.7 event in 1900, in a region where the Caribbean and South American plates meet. The shaking hit a densely populated corridor including the capital, and the gap between the confirmed deaths and USGS modelling underscores the uncertainty over the eventual toll.

What comes next: Search-and-rescue operations continued on 25 June with people reported trapped under collapsed buildings, and the government's count is expected to rise as crews reach La Guaira and other areas. International teams from the US and other countries were mobilising, and authorities were assessing damage to the airport, transport and power networks.

US and Iran set 60-day roadmap; dispute inspections as Israel–Lebanon talks run in Washington

Developing— the roadmap, working groups and Hormuz channel are corroborated across wires, but the inspections timing is openly disputed and the Lebanon-talks outcome was still emerging

The United States and Iran concluded a week of technical negotiations at the Bürgenstock resort in Switzerland, which Iran's delegation said wrapped up on 24 June 2026. The talks followed a high-level committee meeting on 21 June and built on a memorandum of understanding signed virtually by the US and Iranian presidents on 17 June.

According to a joint statement issued by mediators Pakistan and Qatar, the parties agreed on "a roadmap towards reaching a final deal within 60 days." Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi, who led the Iranian delegation, said the sides reached agreement on the framework for future negotiations. Al Jazeera and the Express Tribune reported the creation of working groups covering sanctions termination, nuclear issues, reconstruction and economic development, reporting to a High-Level Committee.

On the Strait of Hormuz, negotiators established a communication line "to avoid incidents and miscommunication" with the stated aim of safe passage for commercial vessels during the 60-day period. Under the 17 June interim arrangement, the strait was set to run toll-free for at least 60 days, and the US lifted its naval blockade in mid-June.

Commercial traffic through the strait rose as the arrangement took effect. CNBC, citing Kpler data, reported tanker transits increasing after the deal's implementation; US Central Command said the waterway never fully closed, reporting 55 merchant ships transited on 20 June. Analysts note traffic remains below pre-war levels of more than 100 ships a day, and reports indicate the central deep-water channel still requires mine-clearing.

France records hottest day on national scale since 1947 as Europe heat peaks

Developing— core figures are reported by multiple outlets and primary agencies, but temperature records are provisional and the red-alert count rose through the week

France recorded its hottest day on a national scale since the measure began in 1947 on Wednesday 24 June 2026, according to Météo-France. The national temperature indicator — an average of readings across roughly 30 stations over 24 hours — reached 30.0°C, surpassing the 29.8°C set the previous day. Both figures are provisional.

For Thursday 25 June, Météo-France placed 72 departments under red ("vigilance rouge") heatwave alert, up from 58 on Wednesday, with the remaining departments under orange or lower levels. The agency said the red alert covered more than 51 million residents.

Météo-France described the episode as an exceptional heatwave on a national scale, of a severity it said could approach that of August 2003, and said 25 June was expected to mark the peak. Afternoon highs of 40–42°C were forecast, with earlier-week readings including 43.6°C at Cazaux (Gironde) and 42.2°C at Nantes.

Authorities reported disruption across France. Thousands of homes experienced power cuts in northern France during the week, more than 2,000 schools closed or adjusted timetables, and operators restricted visiting hours at sites including the Eiffel Tower and the Louvre.

Colombia's electoral council certifies de la Espriella as president-elect after challenge rejected

Verified— the 24 June certification and concession are reported consistently by France 24, Al Jazeera, PBS/AP and NBC News

Colombia's National Electoral Council (CNE) on 24 June 2026 officially declared Abelardo de la Espriella the president-elect for the 2026–2030 term, formalising the result of the 21 June runoff after a plenary session in Bogotá. The resolution also declared José Manuel Restrepo Abondano vice-president-elect.

The certification followed the concession of Iván Cepeda, the candidate of the Petro-aligned left, earlier on 24 June. Cepeda said he had decided to accept the result, framing the move as "an act of democratic responsibility, to contribute to harmony, peace and dialogue."

According to the CNE's official tally, de la Espriella won 12,960,166 votes (49.66%) against Cepeda's 12,708,312 (48.70%), a margin of about 251,854 votes — under one percentage point, and described by outlets as the narrowest in Colombia's recent presidential history.

The CNE rejected the opposition's petitions alleging discrepancies across roughly 33,000 polling stations, ruling the challenges lacked the requirements of the Electoral Code and that discrepancies fell within the normal statistical margin. The National Registry reported a 99.997% match between the election-night preliminary count and the official judge-led count.

Verified— the 24 June conclusion is reported via a CDC briefing and corroborated by Reuters, Al Jazeera and AJMC, though the total case count is given slightly differently across sources

The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) said on 24 June 2026 that it had concluded its public-health response to an outbreak of Andes virus, a type of hantavirus, associated with the expedition cruise ship M/V Hondius. The announcement was made at a CDC media briefing.

According to the CDC, all US citizens identified as having potential exposure aboard the vessel completed a 42-day monitoring period, which ended on 22 June. The agency said none of those monitored developed hantavirus disease and that no cases occurred in the United States as a result of the outbreak.

The CDC said 18 potentially exposed US travellers were repatriated in May and initially observed at the National Quarantine Unit at the University of Nebraska Medical Center, with some later completing monitoring at home. The agency said all remained symptom-free through the end of the observation period.

The outbreak was linked to a voyage that, according to the CDC and New England Journal of Medicine correspondence, ran from Ushuaia, Argentina, in early April through several South Atlantic islands before reaching Tenerife and later Rotterdam. The CDC and the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control have described passengers and crew drawn from more than 20 countries.
