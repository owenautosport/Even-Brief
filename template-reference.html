Stocks steady into Monday after a week defined by a hawkish Fed and falling oil

Verified— the week's index and commodity moves are reported consistently across Reuters, CNBC, TheStreet and Trading Economics

Global equity markets reopen on Monday 22 June after a week shaped by two forces: a hawkish US Federal Reserve and the de-escalation between the United States and Iran. The Federal Open Market Committee, meeting on 16–17 June in the first decision under new chair Kevin Warsh, held the federal funds target at 3.50%–3.75% for a fourth consecutive meeting and dropped its prior easing bias. Updated projections showed nine of the participants who submitted forecasts penciling in at least one rate increase later in 2026.

The decision drove a sharp sell-off on Wednesday 17 June, which equities then partly reversed. In the last full US session on Thursday 18 June, the S&P 500 closed at 7,500.58, up 1.08%; the Nasdaq Composite at 26,517.93, up 1.91%; the Dow Jones Industrial Average at 51,564.70, up 0.14%; and the small-cap Russell 2000 at 2,979.77, up 2.12%. Friday 19 June was the Juneteenth holiday, so US cash markets did not trade.

In Europe, which did trade on Friday 19 June, the FTSE 100 closed at about 10,364, down 0.34%, weighed by mining and precious-metals shares. Germany's DAX ended at 24,986, down 0.16%. In Asia, Japan's Nikkei 225 finished Friday at about 71,250, up 0.28%.

Oil set the tone for the week. Brent crude settled Friday at $80.57 a barrel, up about 0.9% on the day but down roughly 8.5% over the week, as the conditional reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, the lifting of force-majeure declarations by Kuwait and the end of the US naval blockade convinced traders that the disruption which had earlier pushed prices above $120 had passed. West Texas Intermediate traded near $77.50 a barrel. Spot gold eased to about $4,127 an ounce, down roughly 1.5% on Friday, while US Henry Hub natural gas was around $3.10 per million BTU.

The live wildcard into Monday is the Strait of Hormuz. Tehran has said vessels passing through the strait will require mandatory insurance policies — currently free but potentially chargeable later — and major shipping lines have yet to fully resume transits. Talks between the US and Iran in Switzerland were abruptly postponed on Friday, leaving the path from interim agreement to lasting settlement uncertain.

The week ahead is data- and earnings-heavy. FedEx reports on 23 June and Micron on 24 June; May personal-consumption-expenditures inflation and the final first-quarter GDP estimate are due on 25 June; and Nike closes the month with fiscal fourth-quarter results on 30 June.

US and Iran open first round of technical talks in Switzerland as Tehran sets Hormuz insurance rule

Developing— fast-moving; delegation rosters and the MoU signing date are reported with some variation across outlets

Follow-up to earlier coverage

United States and Iranian negotiators held a first round of technical-level talks in Switzerland on Sunday, opening a 60-day window agreed under a memorandum of understanding to convert an interim accord into a durable settlement covering Iran's nuclear programme and the Strait of Hormuz.

Al Jazeera and CNBC reported the meeting took place at the Bürgenstock resort. The US delegation was led by Vice President JD Vance and included envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner; the Iranian side was led by Parliament Speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi. The agenda included the Lebanon ceasefire, the nuclear file and keeping the Strait of Hormuz open. A schedule for the next round was not announced.

Separately, Iran said vessels passing through the Strait of Hormuz would require mandatory insurance, currently issued free of charge by its newly established Persian Gulf Strait Authority but with the right to charge fees later reserved, according to Bloomberg, Lloyd's List and CNBC. The authority requires ships to submit passage requests and follow a prescribed route, a step Tehran presents as reinforcing its claims over the waterway.

Oil eased over the week as shipping conditions improved. Brent crude futures settled at $80.57 a barrel on Friday, on track for a weekly decline of about 8.5%, CNBC reported, paring most of the gains accumulated during the height of the conflict.

"The Iranians, for the second night in a row, did not shoot at any ships in the Strait of Hormuz. So far, they are honoring their end of the commitment."— US Vice President JD Vance, to reporters, 19 June 2026

Poland strips Zelensky of its highest honour over UPA military unit; Ukraine returns the award

Verified— the revocation and the return of awards are reported across CNN, the Kyiv Independent, France 24, The Times of Israel and Al Jazeera

Polish President Karol Nawrocki announced on 19 June 2026 that he had stripped Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky of the Order of the White Eagle, Poland's highest state distinction, which had been awarded to Zelensky in 2023. The move followed Kyiv's decision to name a serving unit of the Armed Forces of Ukraine after the “Heroes of the UPA” — the WWII-era Ukrainian Insurgent Army.

In a video address, Nawrocki said the decision was taken “in view of President Volodymyr Zelensky's consent to one of the units of the Armed Forces of Ukraine being named after the Heroes of the UPA, and following consultations with the Chapter.” He said the UPA “remains above all a formation responsible for cruel crimes against the citizens of the Polish Republic during World War II,” adding that “we have supported and continue to support Ukraine because we know that Russian aggression poses a threat.”

On 20 June, Zelensky said he had returned the order by post, sharing photographs taken at a post office showing a mailing form addressed to the Chancellery of the President of Poland. “We believed that the Order of the White Eagle, awarded in 2023, was meant for the Ukrainian People and our army,” Zelensky said in a statement reported by Al Jazeera and The Times of Israel.

Several senior Ukrainian officials said they would relinquish Polish honours in solidarity. Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha, military intelligence chief Kyrylo Budanov and Ukraine's ambassador to Warsaw Vasyl Bodnar each announced they were returning Polish awards on 20 June. Budanov said he “cannot stand aside and simply watch as the flywheel of hatred is unreasonably and artificially spun against our citizens.”

Health

WHO warns of “missing” Ebola cases in DR Congo as official tallies diverge across agencies

Developing— case counts are being revised and harmonised; agencies disagree on totals and the live figure is not independently verified

Follow-up to earlier coverage

Follow-up: One month after the Bundibugyo-virus Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo was declared a public health emergency of international concern, the World Health Organization says the epidemic is still spreading and that surveillance is not capturing every case. “That means we are missing cases,” WHO incident manager Dr Marie-Roseline Belizaire told CBS News on 16 June, describing continued community deaths and undetected transmission in affected provinces.

The warning lands alongside a notable wrinkle in the data: as of late June, WHO, the U.S. Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and the European Centre for Disease Prevention and Control have published materially different running totals, and the count has at one point been revised sharply downward rather than up. The figures below should therefore be read as agency snapshots in flux, not a single settled number.

The most recent national tally widely cited is from the DRC Ministry of Health and reported by the ECDC: as of 15 June, 837 confirmed cases and 196 confirmed deaths in the DRC, with 376 people hospitalised in isolation. WHO’s own Disease Outbreak News (DON607) carried a lower figure dated earlier in the month — 676 confirmed cases and 136 deaths in the DRC as of 10 June — because the published bulletins reflect different cut-off dates and verification stages.

Earlier in June, the picture was murkier still. CIDRAP reported that WHO and the CDC had cut the official count from “nearly 1,000” cases to 321 confirmed cases and 48 deaths in the DRC, with a further 116 suspected, attributing the swing to laboratory reclassification as a backlog of samples was tested. Relief groups quoted by CIDRAP called the shifting figures “part of a broader, chaotic picture of an outbreak that may have been simmering for months.” Which adjustment supersedes which is not fully reconciled in the public bulletins.

The outbreak is concentrated in north-eastern DRC. Ituri province accounts for the large majority of confirmed cases, with North Kivu and a small number in South Kivu also affected; Uganda has reported 19 confirmed cases and two deaths, described by WHO as imported cases and secondary transmission among contacts and health workers. The U.S. has pledged more than $220 million toward the response.

Japan proposes G7 jointly stockpile critical minerals; leaders set a 60% cap on reliance on any single supplier

Verified— the proposal and the summit declaration are reported consistently across Reuters, Nikkei Asia, Bloomberg and the Élysée primary text

Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi proposed at the Group of Seven summit in Evian, France, that member countries jointly stockpile critical minerals, including rare earths, under what Tokyo calls a "Joint Stockpiling Cooperation Initiative." She put the plan to leaders at a working dinner on 15 June, the first day of the summit, according to Japanese government and wire accounts.

Under the Japanese proposal, each G7 member and like-minded partner would build a national stockpile covering at least 90 days of demand, and coordinate with the International Energy Agency (IEA) to release reserves jointly in the event of a supply disruption. Japan, which already operates a stockpiling system, offered to help other members set up their own.

The leaders established a non-binding Critical Minerals Resilience and Production Alliance, backed by a coordination platform drawing on the IEA and the OECD, and named lithium and nickel as the two pilot metals for joint mechanisms.

22 June 2026 | Aarhus, Denmark
Science Climate

Aarhus researchers report intense UV light can break down PFAS without added chemicals

Verified— named institution, researchers and peer-reviewed journal (Environmental Science & Technology) confirmed across the university release, ScienceDaily and the journal record

Researchers at Aarhus University in Denmark report that intense ultraviolet light can break down PFAS “forever chemicals” in water without any added chemical reagents, by generating highly reactive hydrogen radicals directly from water molecules. The findings were published in the peer-reviewed journal Environmental Science & Technology and summarised by ScienceDaily on 16 June 2026.

In the team’s account, ultraviolet light at wavelengths below 300 nanometres (the UV-C range) splits water to produce hydrogen radicals. These radicals attack the carbon–fluorine bonds that make PFAS so stable, progressively stripping away fluorine atoms and breaking the molecules into smaller, less persistent substances. The study identifies hydrogen radicals — rather than hydrated electrons, the species earlier work had emphasised — as the dominant driver of the reaction.

The work is laboratory-stage and the breakdown is partial. For GenX, a PFOA substitute, the researchers reported up to 49.1% degradation and 21.2% defluorination within five hours under simulated solar light in a catalyst-free setup. The team notes the process is relatively slow and that intermediate compounds can form during treatment, and presents the result as a mechanistic insight rather than a finished treatment technology.

The authors listed on the paper are Lu Bai, Shuang Luo, Jan Thøgersen, Xingaoyuan Xiong, Zheng Guo and Zongsu Wei, based at Aarhus University’s Centre for Water Technology and Department of Biological and Chemical Engineering. The article, titled “Mechanistic Insights into Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substance (PFAS) Photolysis under Intensified Simulated Solar Light,” carries DOI 10.1021/acs.est.5c16178.

Why it matters: PFAS are a class of thousands of synthetic chemicals valued for their resistance to heat, water and grease, but their carbon–fluorine bonds make them persist in the environment and accumulate in water and the body. PFAS have been detected in drinking water across all 50 US states and have been linked in regulatory and health literature to certain cancers, immune effects and developmental harm. Most existing treatments capture or concentrate PFAS rather than destroying them, so methods that actually break the molecules apart are of particular interest.

What comes next: the open questions are whether the approach can be scaled beyond the lab — UV-C generation is energy-intensive, and reactor design would have to ensure light reaches the contaminated water — and how to push degradation closer to complete mineralisation while controlling the intermediate compounds that form. The researchers describe the result as a starting point for designing UV reactors, not an immediate fix for PFAS contamination.

22 June 2026 | Sydney
Health Science

Review concludes nicotine vapes are likely to cause lung and oral cancer

Developing— the publication and its authorship are confirmed, but the central claim is a hazard judgement rather than a measured human cancer rate, and outside experts dispute its strength

A research review led by UNSW Sydney has concluded that nicotine-based e-cigarettes are likely to cause lung and oral cancers in people who use them. The paper, titled "The carcinogenicity of e-cigarettes: a qualitative risk assessment," was published in the journal Carcinogenesis on 30 March 2026 and drew a fresh wave of coverage around 19–21 June. The authors state that nicotine-based e-cigarettes are "likely to be carcinogenic to humans who use them, causing an indeterminate burden of oral cancer and lung cancer."

The assessment was led by UNSW cancer researcher Adjunct Professor Bernard W Stewart, with co-authors drawn from the University of Queensland, Flinders University, the University of Sydney, Royal North Shore Hospital, The Prince Charles Hospital and Sunshine Coast University Hospital. The team, which included pharmacists, epidemiologists, a thoracic surgeon and public-health researchers, weighed a wide body of international research rather than running a new trial.

The review draws on three strands of evidence: human biomarkers showing DNA damage, oxidative stress and tissue inflammation in users; animal experiments in which mice exposed to e-cigarette aerosol developed lung tumours; and laboratory studies of cellular damage and disrupted biological pathways. The authors also point to carcinogenic compounds in the aerosol, including nicotine-derived nitrosamines, volatile organic compounds and metals released from heating coils.

The authors are explicit about the limits of the work. "Our assessment is qualitative and does not involve a numerical estimate of cancer risk or burden," the paper states; the team describes the result as the most definitive determination to date that vapers face an increased cancer risk, while noting that precise risk levels await longer-term human studies. Co-author Associate Professor Freddy Sitas argued that, with e-cigarettes introduced about 20 years ago, "we should not wait another 80 years to decide what to do."

Several outside experts pushed back. In reactions gathered by the UK Science Media Centre, Professor Lion Shahab of University College London said the narrative review "makes extraordinary claims that are not borne out by the data," and Professor Peter Hajek of Queen Mary University of London called the conclusions "misleading," stressing that "it is the dose that makes the poison" and that vapers receive a small fraction of the carcinogens in tobacco smoke. Dr Stephen Burgess of the MRC Biostatistics Unit said evidence linking the proposed mechanisms to a quantifiable rise in cancer risk "is absent."

Why it matters: E-cigarettes have been widely promoted as less harmful than smoking and as a quitting aid, and regulators including England's NHS have treated them as a lower-risk option for established smokers. A peer-reviewed judgement that vaping is likely carcinogenic, even without a measured population cancer rate, feeds directly into debates over marketing, age limits and product regulation; Australia restricted disposable vapes and limited therapeutic vapes to pharmacies in 2023.

What comes next: The review does not establish how large any cancer risk is, and that gap will only close as long-running cohort studies of vapers mature. Watch for responses from cancer agencies and tobacco-control bodies, and for whether regulators cite the assessment. The relative-harm comparison with smoking — central to the experts' objections — remains contested and is the key claim still to be resolved.
