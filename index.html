A global sell-off centred on memory-chip and AI-related shares extended into a second session on Tuesday, 23 June 2026. On Wall Street the Nasdaq Composite closed down 2.21% at 25,587.04, the S&P 500 fell 1.44%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average ended little changed, down 0.09% at 51,666.84.

US and Iran give conflicting accounts of nuclear-inspector access after first Switzerland talks

Disputed— US and Iranian officials publicly conflict on whether IAEA inspectors will reach the war-damaged nuclear sites; the scope of any agreement is unresolved.

Follow-up to earlier coverage

The United States and Iran offered conflicting accounts of nuclear-inspector access following the first round of direct talks aimed at ending the war between the two countries, held on 21–22 June 2026 at the Bürgenstock resort near Lucerne, Switzerland.

US Vice-President JD Vance told reporters at the Bürgenstock on 22 June that "the Iranians have agreed to invite IAEA inspectors back into their country," describing it as "a major milestone for the American people and the first step in permanently denuclearising or permanently ending a nuclear weapons programme in Iran." Vance said coordination between Iran, the United States and the International Atomic Energy Agency could take place "this week, maybe as soon as today."

Iran's foreign ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei told a news conference in Tehran on 23 June that no visit had been scheduled for IAEA inspectors to examine the nuclear sites struck by the United States, and said there was no protocol for such inspections. Baghaei said Iran had neither held a meeting with IAEA chief Rafael Grossi in Switzerland nor planned for the agency to inspect its damaged facilities, while inspections of sites such as the Bushehr power plant would continue.

The talks, mediated by Qatar and Pakistan, produced what the mediators called "encouraging progress" and a roadmap toward a final deal within 60 days. Negotiators created a high-level committee for political oversight and working groups on Iran's nuclear programme, sanctions, and a monitoring and dispute-resolution mechanism. Both sides said substantial differences remained on nuclear issues, sanctions and regional security.

Alan Greenspan, US Federal Reserve chair from 1987 to 2006, dies at 100

Verified— the death, age and cause are confirmed across multiple major outlets.

Alan Greenspan, who served as chairman of the US Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, died on 22 June 2026 at his home in Washington, D.C., at the age of 100. His wife of 29 years, NBC News correspondent Andrea Mitchell, said in a statement that the cause was complications from Parkinson's disease.

Greenspan was born on 6 March 1926 in New York City. He studied music at the Juilliard School and played jazz saxophone and clarinet before earning bachelor's and master's degrees in economics from New York University and beginning doctoral study at Columbia University. In the 1950s he joined the circle around the novelist Ayn Rand and became an advocate of laissez-faire economics.

He chaired the Council of Economic Advisers under President Gerald Ford from 1974 to 1977. President Ronald Reagan nominated him to lead the Federal Reserve in 1987; the Senate confirmed him on 11 August 1987. He was reappointed at four-year intervals and served under Reagan, George H.W. Bush, Bill Clinton and George W. Bush, retiring on 31 January 2006.

During his tenure he was widely referred to as "the Maestro," a label drawn from the title of Bob Woodward's 2000 book. On 5 December 1996, in a speech at the American Enterprise Institute, he posed a question about "irrational exuberance" in asset prices that became one of his most-cited remarks.

Why it matters: Greenspan's tenure of about 18 years and five months was the second-longest in the central bank's history, behind William McChesney Martin Jr., who served from 1951 to 1970. His decisions on interest rates shaped US monetary conditions during a period that included an expansion lasting from 1991 to 2001.

After the 2007–08 financial crisis, Greenspan drew criticism over his support for financial deregulation. Testifying before the House Committee on Oversight and Government Reform on 23 October 2008, he said he had found "a flaw" in his assumption that lending institutions would act to protect shareholders' equity. The Economist later described him as "a naive believer in market efficiency." Defenders point to the long expansion of the 1990s.

What comes next: tributes followed. Former Fed chair Ben Bernanke, his successor, said "we are still learning from him." The Federal Reserve said his "contributions to monetary policy and economic thought left a lasting mark on this institution." Survivors include his wife, Andrea Mitchell; memorial arrangements had not been announced as of 23 June.

NATO heads to Ankara summit on 5% spending pledge as Czech PM excludes President Pavel from delegation

Developing— the summit dates, 5% structure, delegation list and Pavel's filing are corroborated, but the Constitutional Court has not ruled and the delegation may still change.

NATO's 32 member states are preparing for a summit in Ankara, Türkiye, on 7–8 July 2026, the alliance's second hosted by Türkiye after Istanbul in 2004. Secretary General Mark Rutte will preside, with President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan as host. The gathering is being framed around delivery rather than new pledges.

The central benchmark is the commitment agreed at The Hague in June 2025, under which allies pledged to invest 5% of GDP in defence by 2035 — at least 3.5% for core military requirements and up to 1.5% for defence- and security-related spending such as infrastructure and industrial capacity. The pledge built on the alliance's 2014 target of 2% of GDP.

Allied defence ministers held their final pre-summit meeting in Brussels on 18 June 2026. Rutte said the ministers had made "good progress" and that NATO needed to "turn the cash into combat-ready capabilities, and fast." Against that backdrop, the Czech government said its delegation to Ankara would comprise Prime Minister Andrej Babiš, Defence Minister Jaromír Zuna and Foreign Minister Petr Macinka. President Petr Pavel was not included.

Babiš described the reasoning as "purely practical," saying the government would need to defend its defence-spending plans at the summit, an area he said falls to his cabinet, and rejecting the suggestion that the government was "forbidding" the president from acting. Pavel responded by filing a competence action at the Constitutional Court, arguing that representing the state abroad is a presidential power under Article 63 of the Czech Constitution.

23 June 2026 | Cambridge, Massachusetts
Science

Astronomers report the earliest known 'flickering' quasar, 850 million years after the Big Bang

Verified— object identity, redshift, mass, instrument, journal and named authors are confirmed across the Nature Astronomy paper and several outlets; the maturation-timeline inference is single-sourced.

A team led by researchers at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology reports the detection of brightness variability, or "flickering," in a quasar seen as it appeared roughly 850 million years after the Big Bang. The object, designated J0439+1634, is described by the authors as the earliest flickering quasar identified to date. The findings were published on 8 June 2026 in the journal Nature Astronomy.

A quasar is the luminous core of a galaxy in which gas falling toward a supermassive black hole is heated and radiates across the electromagnetic spectrum. J0439+1634 sits at a redshift of z = 6.51 and is gravitationally lensed by a foreground galaxy, which magnifies its light. After correcting for that lensing, the team estimates the black hole's mass at about 630 million times that of the Sun.

The variability signal was drawn from about 14 years of infrared observations by NASA's NEOWISE mission. Over that period the quasar's output changed by approximately 20 per cent, fluctuating without a fixed pattern. Lead author Gene Leung said the flickering arises from changes in the rate at which gas is fed into the black hole.

The team interprets the character of the variability as evidence that the gas-feeding structure is a geometrically thin, flat accretion disk, similar to those observed around black holes in the present-day universe. According to the authors, current models had generally expected black holes still in a rapid early-growth phase to host thicker, more turbulent disks.

The conclusions rest on a single object, and the disk geometry is inferred from variability rather than imaged directly.

Why it matters: if the interpretation holds, it indicates that at least one black hole had completed its most energetic growth phase and reached a comparatively stable feeding state before the era of cosmic dawn ended — which, the authors say, would push the period of most intense black-hole assembly to earlier times.

What comes next: the researchers point to wide-field surveys, including NASA's Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope, as a way to test whether J0439+1634 is typical; independent confirmation would require detecting flickering in additional quasars at comparable redshifts.

Each Key Facts bullet (in the sidebar) is a link — tap it and the article scrolls to and briefly highlights the paragraph that fact comes from.

❝

Dated pull-quotes & sources

Every quote names who said it and when. The sidebar's Sources list links out to the original reporting and, where possible, the primary document.

🖼️

Licence-safe images

Photos are public-domain or open-licensed (e.g. NASA, USGS, CDC, Wikimedia Commons), captioned with their source and licence — never lifted from the outlets we cite.

📌

Sticky facts rail

On longer stories the Key Facts and Sources panel follows you down the page, so the supporting detail stays in view while you read.

Markets pages
📈

Overview · Movers · Calendar · Stocks

Overview is the written markets analysis. Movers is an at-a-glance dashboard of the biggest gainers and losers across the US, UK and Europe with a major-index strip. Calendar lists the month's earnings, IPOs, dividends, central-bank meetings and data releases. Stocks is a TradingView-powered page: search by ticker or company name, browse the stock grid, and tap any stock to open a large interactive chart for it — it needs an internet connection to load.

Display settings
◐

Dark / Light theme

Switch the whole briefing between dark and light from the bottom of the menu; your choice is remembered.

◎

Focus mode

Turn it on to spotlight one paragraph at a time — everything else dims and the headline pins to the top as you scroll. A Show full article button at the end restores the whole piece. It always starts off.
